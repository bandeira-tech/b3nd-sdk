# 5. The Limits of Presence

Everything in the first four chapters runs on air.

Alice and Bob decide on dinner — face to face. The politician speaks at the
podium — in a room full of listeners. The doctor prescribes medicine — patient
sitting across the desk. Carol witnesses the handshake — standing right there.

Air is a beautiful medium. It's immediate. It carries tone, emotion, nuance. It
lets you see the speaker's face, read their body language, respond in real time.
For thousands of years, it was the only medium humans had.

But air has physics. And those physics have limits.

## Air Dissipates

Sound fades. What was said at dinner last Tuesday? You remember the gist, but
the exact words are gone. Was the agreement "I'll pay you back next week" or
"I'll pay you back next month"? Neither party can be certain. The medium erases
itself.

For casual agreements, that's fine. For a business deal, a treaty, a promise
that must hold for decades — it's fatal. The medium's ephemerality means that
agreements degrade over time. Witnesses forget. Details blur. Disputes become
irresolvable because nobody has the original words.

This is not a flaw. It's physics. Sound is a compression wave in air. It
propagates, reflects, and dissipates. It does not persist. No amount of wanting
it to persist will change this.

## Air Doesn't Travel Far

Alice is in Lisbon. Bob is in Tokyo. They need to make a deal.

Air can't carry the message. The medium's range is a room. Maybe a hillside if
you shout. Maybe a few miles if you use drums or horns. But the Atlantic Ocean
is 5,500 kilometers, and air doesn't bridge it.

Every face-to-face protocol in Chapters 1 through 4 assumes the parties are in
the same room. When they're not, the protocol breaks. You can't propose,
counter-propose, and converge on an agreement if you can't hear each other.

## Air Can't Address

You can speak to a crowd, but you can't direct your voice to one specific person
across town. The medium has no routing. Sound goes outward from you in all
directions. Everyone nearby hears it — whether they're the intended audience or
not.

If you want to send a private message to one person who isn't in the room, air
alone can't do it. You could shout their name, but that also announces to
everyone else that you have a message for them. The medium has no concept of
"addressed delivery."

## Air Reveals Everything Nearby

You can whisper, but anyone close enough will hear. You can close a door, but
that only works if you have a room. If you're in an open space, a crowd, a
marketplace — privacy requires physical distance from everyone you don't want to
hear.

The medium has no built-in selective privacy. It doesn't have "channels" or
"addressed envelopes." Everything within earshot is public, and the boundary of
earshot is fuzzy and unreliable.

## The Physics Force the Invention

Each of these limits is a physics problem. And each one forces the invention of
a corresponding solution — not because someone clever designed it, but because
communication must continue even when air fails.

| Air's limit              | The problem                                          | The forced invention                                                                       |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Dissipation**          | Agreements degrade over time                         | **Writing** — mark the words on something that persists                                    |
| **Limited range**        | Parties aren't in the same room                      | **Carriers** — give the message to something that travels: a runner, a horse, a ship       |
| **No addressing**        | Can't direct a message to a specific person far away | **Addresses** — label the message with who it's for, so the carrier knows where to take it |
| **No selective privacy** | Everyone nearby hears everything                     | **Seals and encryption** — make the content readable only by the intended recipient        |

These inventions aren't technology for its own sake. They are the minimum viable
answers to: "how do I keep having this conversation when the person isn't in
front of me?"

You don't write a letter because writing is better than talking. You write
because the person isn't in front of you.

You don't sign a document because signatures are fun. You sign because the
agreement needs to outlast the handshake — and the medium of air won't preserve
it.

You don't seal an envelope because you enjoy wax. You seal it because the medium
of paper passes through hands you don't control, and you need the content to
stay private.

You don't use a computer because it's simpler. You use it because 10,000 people
can't stand in the same room, and you need the conversation to happen at global
scale in real time.

Every "technology" in this book is an answer to the same question: **how do I
have this conversation when the current medium can no longer carry it?**

## The Pivot

This is the pivot of the book.

In Part I, we established the human foundations: agreement, trust, identity,
witnesses, formality. All in spoken dialogue. All face to face.

From here forward, every chapter walks the three-layer stack. Each concept
starts in speech, moves to paper, and arrives at digital. The pattern repeats:

1. Here's how it works when you're in the room (speech, air)
2. Here's what changes when the message must travel (paper, physical carriers)
3. Here's what changes again when the message must be instant and global
   (digital, networks)

The conversation stays the same. Alice and Bob still need to agree on dinner.
Carol still needs to witness. The doctor still needs to prescribe. The
politician still needs to commit.

What changes is the medium. And from the medium's physics — its gifts and its
limits — emerge every protocol feature you'll encounter in the rest of this
book. Addresses, signatures, encryption, inboxes, consensus chains, validators,
confirmers, nodes. Each one is a solution to a specific limit of a specific
medium.

Not arbitrary. Not invented. Forced.

Turn the page. The medium changes. The message is born.

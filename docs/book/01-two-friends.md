# 1. Two Friends

Alice and Bob are deciding where to eat.

Alice says: "How about pizza?"

Bob says: "Sure, let's go."

That's it. An agreement was reached. Two messages. One outcome. A protocol
completed — though neither Alice nor Bob would call it that.

## The Anatomy of the Simplest Agreement

Break down what just happened:

1. **Alice proposed.** She put an option on the table.
2. **Bob accepted.** He endorsed her proposal.
3. **A shared understanding was created.** Both now know: we're getting pizza.
   Neither can reasonably claim otherwise.

The sequence matters. Bob can't say "sure" before Alice proposes — there's
nothing to agree to. Alice can't declare they're going for pizza without Bob's
response — that's a command, not an agreement. The meaning lives in the _order_
of the messages, not in either message alone.

This is the simplest possible protocol. A proposal and an acceptance, in that
order. From this seed, everything in this book grows.

## What's Inside a Message

"How about pizza?" is four words. But notice how much those four words carry.

It's a **proposal** — Alice is suggesting an option. It's a **test of Bob's
mood** — she's checking if he's open to her idea. It's a **signal of
preference** — Alice wants pizza. And it's a **willingness to negotiate** — she
said "how about," not "we're going to." She left room for Bob to push back.

Four words. Four simultaneous meanings. And neither Alice nor Bob had to think
about this. They just talked.

Now consider a different scene. A boss walks past an employee's desk, glances at
their screen, and says: "Interesting work."

Is that praise? A warning that the boss noticed? A prelude to reassignment? An
invitation to present at the next meeting? The same two words carry completely
different meanings depending on tone, history, the relationship between the two
people, and what happened last week.

This is the first deep truth about messages: **they compress unlimited
complexity.** A few words at a dinner table can encode respect, threat,
validation, and commentary all at once. This isn't a limitation of language —
it's the fundamental nature of communication. Meaning is dense. A message is
small. What it carries is vast.

Remember this. It will matter enormously when we get to digital systems.

## Adding Complexity

Back to dinner. Alice proposes pizza. But this time Bob says: "I had pizza
yesterday. How about Thai?"

Three messages now:

1. Alice proposes pizza
2. Bob rejects and counter-proposes Thai
3. Alice says: "Thai works. Let's go."

Same structure — proposal, response, resolution — but now with negotiation. Bob
didn't just accept or reject. He rejected _and_ proposed an alternative in the
same message. One turn doing the work of two.

Now add a third friend. Carol joins and says: "I'm vegetarian — can we make sure
there are options for me?"

The protocol adapts without anyone redesigning it. Carol introduced a
**constraint**. The group now has to find a restaurant that satisfies Alice's
preference, Bob's counter-proposal, and Carol's requirement. Three parties.
Three messages' worth of constraints. The conversation continues until all three
are satisfied.

Notice what we have, and we haven't left the dinner table:

- **Multi-party negotiation.** More than two people, each with their own
  interests.
- **Constraints.** Carol's vegetarian requirement limits the solution space.
- **Counter-proposals.** Bob didn't just say yes or no — he offered an
  alternative.
- **Consensus.** The group converges on a restaurant that works for everyone.

This is the same structure that runs a corporate board meeting, a parliamentary
session, a trade negotiation between countries. The complexity scales, but the
pattern doesn't change: people take turns speaking, each turn adds information
or constraints, and the group converges on an outcome through the sequence of
turns.

## The Three Sources of Meaning

Why does this work? Why can a few exchanged sentences create a binding
agreement?

Because meaning in dialogue comes from three things, always:

**The sequence.** The order of messages matters. "How about pizza?" followed by
"Sure" is a deal. "Sure" followed by "How about pizza?" is nonsense. Reverse the
order and the meaning dissolves. The sequence IS the agreement.

**The speakers.** Who says what matters. If a stranger walked up and said "Sure,
let's go" — Alice wouldn't go to pizza with them. The identity of each speaker
gives their words weight and relevance.

**The setting.** Where the conversation happens matters. Friends at a bar
deciding on dinner — that's casual, low-stakes, easily reversed. The same words
exchanged by diplomats at a summit, or by lawyers in a mediation room, carry the
weight of institutions and consequences. We'll explore this deeply in the next
chapter.

These three — sequence, speaker, setting — are the only tools humans have ever
needed to create agreements. Every protocol in history, from a handshake to a
blockchain, is built from some combination of these three. And every chapter in
this book is about how they transform as the medium of communication changes.

## The Dinner Table as Protocol

One more thing about the dinner table. Nobody planned this protocol. Nobody
wrote down the rules for "how to decide where to eat." And yet Alice, Bob, and
Carol executed it flawlessly. They proposed, counter-proposed, introduced
constraints, negotiated, and converged — all without a manual.

They could do this because they are human beings who have been communicating
since before they could walk. The rules of dialogue — take turns, listen before
responding, acknowledge what others say, work toward shared understanding — are
older than written language, older than civilization, older than agriculture.

When we move to letters and then to digital systems, we will not leave these
rules behind. We will carry them forward, intact. What changes is the medium —
the physical substance through which the messages travel. What stays the same is
the conversation.

Even the simplest dialogue already demonstrates that a small number of messages
between parties can express, negotiate, and finalize agreements of arbitrary
complexity. This power doesn't come from the words being clever. It comes from
the sequence, the speakers, and the setting.

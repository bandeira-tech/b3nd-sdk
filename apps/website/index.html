The model we inherited

The internet we use today is built on a simple assumption: users connect to servers, and servers hold everything. Your photos, your messages, your purchases, your history‚Äîall stored on computers you don't control, managed by companies whose interests aren't yours.

This made sense when storage was expensive and connections were slow. But that was decades ago. Now we carry supercomputers in our pockets and pay for bandwidth we barely use.

The client-server model persists not because it's the best way to build software, but because it's the best way to build platforms that extract value from users.

B3nd asks: what if we designed from first principles for today's technology and tomorrow's users?

What becomes possible

When you change how data works, you change what you can build

üí∞ Direct value exchange

When there's no platform in the middle, advertisers can pay users directly for attention. Creators can sell directly to audiences. The 30% platform fee disappears.

Example: An app where you see ads and get paid‚Äînot the platform. The advertiser gets verified human attention, you get compensated for yours.

üîê Real privacy

When data is encrypted before it leaves your device, privacy isn't a policy‚Äîit's math. The network can't read what it can't decrypt.

Example: Messages that are literally unreadable by anyone except sender and recipient. Not "we promise not to look"‚Äîactually impossible to look.

üîÑ Portable identity

When your data lives at addresses you control, switching apps doesn't mean starting over. Your history, your relationships, your content‚Äîit all comes with you.

Example: Leave a social network but keep your followers. Switch note-taking apps but keep your notes. The data is yours wherever you go.

üåê Distributed infrastructure

When storage and messaging are protocols instead of services, anyone can run the infrastructure. Local businesses can serve local traffic. The network becomes the community.

Example: A coffee shop that runs a node, serves content to customers, and gets paid for the bandwidth. Infrastructure as community service.

How it works

Simple concepts, powerful implications

Addressable Data

Every piece of data has a URI‚Äîan address that belongs to whoever controls the key. Like URLs for the web, but for everything.

mutable://accounts/{key}/profile

Signed Messages

Every write is cryptographically signed. The network doesn't need to trust you‚Äîit can verify mathematically that you authorized the action.

{ auth: [{ pubkey, signature }], payload }

Client-Side Encryption

Private data is encrypted before it leaves your device. The network stores bytes it can't read. Privacy is structural, not promised.
